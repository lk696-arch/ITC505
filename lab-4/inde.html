Overview

The Public Domain Review is an online journal showcasing works which have entered the public domain. It was co-founded by Jonathan Gray and Adam Green. It was launched on January 1, 2011, to coincide with Public Domain Day.

The Review aims to raise awareness of the public domain by promoting public domain works from across the web, including from Europeana, the Internet Archive, and Wikimedia Commons. As well as curated collections of public domain images, texts, and films, it features longer essays from contemporary writers, scholars, and public intellectuals.

The Guardian reviewed it as “magnificent … a model of digital curation,” an interview in Vice labelled it “beautifully curated,” and The A.V. Club described it as “endlessly and deeply absorbing.”

Contributors and Content

Contributors of articles have included Julian Barnes, Frank Delaney, Jack Zipes, Richard Hamblyn, Philipp Blom, and Arika Okrent. In addition to the thematic essays, a monthly “Curator’s Choice” series highlights professional curators’ essays about material from their cultural institutions.

The Review published its first print anthology in late 2014, a collection of 34 essays published online during 2011–13. It was reviewed as “an incredible collection of esoterica” by The Paris Review, and featured as one of Wired’s best science books of 2014. A second volume was published in 2015.

Funding

It was originally launched with seed funding from the Open Knowledge Foundation and the Shuttleworth Foundation before becoming an independent community interest company supported by its readers.

Addendum: My Presentational CSS Choices

Font choices

I paired a readable sans-serif for body text with a classic serif for headings to create a clear hierarchy and improve long-form readability. Body/UI uses Inter (with a broad character set and good hinting); headings use Merriweather for contrast. Both load from Google Fonts with display=swap to avoid invisible text.

Type scale & sizes

  • Main text is set at 1rem (16px base) with a comfortable 1.7 line-height.
  • Headings use a responsive clamp scale (e.g., h1: clamp(2rem, 3vw, 2.75rem)) so they look good on laptop screens and above.
  • Maximum line length is limited via a content width to ~70–80 characters for readability.

Color choices

I used a teal accent (--teal-700) for headings and interactive states, with neutral grays for body text and backgrounds. Contrast ratios meet or exceed WCAG AA for text on the light background.

Spacing, margins, and padding

  • Global rhythm via --space-* tokens; consistent top/bottom margins on headings and paragraphs.
  • Generous page padding and a centered content column improve scannability on laptop-sized viewports.
  • Lists and references get slightly tighter spacing to keep dense sections compact but legible.

Modern CSS & platform diversity

  • Custom properties for theme tokens; clamp() for fluid type; :focus-visible for accessible focus rings.
  • Reduced motion respected via @media (prefers-reduced-motion).
  • System-font fallbacks ensure acceptable rendering if web fonts fail.

HTML abuse

I removed all presentational CSS from the HTML (Lab 2 used an inline <style> block). Styles now live in styles.css. The title remains a semantic <h1>, and section headings are <h2>. No heading levels are chosen for “how they look.” Spacing is controlled with margins in CSS (no <br> for layout).
